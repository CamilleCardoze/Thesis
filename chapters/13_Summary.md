# Summary

This paper studied the regeneration of a pharmaceutical waste solvent by batch extractive distillation. The main objective was to recover an IPA-rich distillate with at least 99.75 mol% IPA from an IPA–water mixture using DMSO as an extractive solvent. This separation was necessary because IPA and water form a minimum-boiling azeotrope, making conventional batch distillation unsuitable for reaching the required product purity. ChemCAD simulations were used to analyze the behavior of the IPA–water–DMSO system and to determine suitable operating parameters for the batch extractive distillation process. The effects of feed stage, reflux ratio, feed rate, number of stages and feed temperature were investigated. Based on the selected operating conditions, the main distillation step achieved a maximum IPA purity of 99.856 mol%, meeting the required specification. The main IPA distillation lasted 9.05 hours.

After the main IPA recovery step, additional distillation stages were considered to recover remaining IPA, water, and DMSO from the bottom mixture. These stages improved the overall material recovery of the process and reduced the amount of valuable material discarded as waste. Two material recycling strategies were compared. The preferred option was the reintegration of the water/IPA run-off into the following batch, while collecting the water/DMSO run-off for separate treatment. This method gave similar product purity, slightly higher IPA recovery, shorter operating time, and better process stability.

The energy demand of the process was also evaluated. For the complete process, the total energy requirement was approximately 16.6025 GJ, corresponding to an estimated cost of 278.92 USD. To reduce this energy demand, two heat-recycling alternatives were studied: a pre-heater method and a heat pump method. The pre-heater was simpler and required a much lower additional investment, while the heat pump required more equipment but provided much larger energy savings, with an estimated net saving of 159,626.13 USD and a payback time of 2.57 years.

The main process equipment was also sized. The packed distillation column, tanks, condenser, reboiler, heat-recovery equipment, and vacuum pump were selected or preliminarily designed. The selected tank geometry had a diameter of 2.00 m, a height of 2.50 m, and a total volume of 7.85 m³. A preliminary cost estimation was carried out for the base distillation system, and the estimated installed cost of the base system was 412,669.94 USD.

Overall, the results show that batch extractive distillation with DMSO is a feasible method for regenerating high-purity IPA from pharmaceutical waste solvent streams, while allowing partial recovery and the reuse of valuable run-off streams and heat integration. Therefore, the complete process provides a technically viable and potentially sustainable solution for solvent regeneration, waste reduction, and energy-efficient recovery of IPA.
