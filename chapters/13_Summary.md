# Summary

This paper studied the regeneration of a pharmaceutical waste solvent by batch extractive distillation. The objective was to recover an IPA-rich distillate with at least 99.75 mol% IPA from an IPA–water mixture using DMSO as an extractive solvent. Since IPA and water form a minimum-boiling azeotrope, conventional batch distillation cannot reach the required purity. ChemCAD simulations were used to study the IPA–water–DMSO system and evaluate the effects of feed stage, reflux ratio, feed rate, number of stages, and feed temperature. Under the selected operating conditions, the main distillation step reached a maximum IPA purity of 99.856 mol% and lasted 9.05 hours.

Additional distillation stages were considered to recover remaining IPA, water, and DMSO from the bottom mixture, improving material recovery and reducing waste. Two recycling strategies were compared. The preferred option was the reintegration of the water/IPA run-off into the following batch, while collecting the water/DMSO run-off separately. This method provided similar product purity, slightly higher IPA recovery, shorter operating time, and better process stability.

The total energy requirement of the complete process was approximately 16.6025 GJ, corresponding to an estimated cost of 278.92 USD. Two heat-recycling alternatives were also studied. The pre-heater method was simpler and required much lower investment, while the heat pump method involved more equipment but offered greater energy savings, with an estimated net saving of 159,626.13 USD and a payback time of 2.57 years.

The main process equipment was preliminarily sized, including the packed column, tanks, condenser, reboiler, heat-recovery equipment, and vacuum pump. The selected tank geometry had a diameter of 2.00 m, a height of 2.50 m, and a total volume of 7.85 m³. The estimated installed cost of the base distillation system was 412,669.94 USD.

Overall, the results show that batch extractive distillation with DMSO is a feasible method for regenerating high-purity IPA from pharmaceutical waste solvent streams. The proposed process supports solvent recovery, run-off reuse, waste reduction, and improved energy efficiency, making it a technically viable and potentially sustainable regeneration solution.
